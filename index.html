Shamanic Retreat

If Lake Baikal is the pearl of Siberia, then Olkhon Island is the heart of Baikal. Stretching 70 kilometres long and 15 kilometres wide, Olkhon is the largest island on Lake Baikal. For centuries, Olkhon has been inhabited by the native Buryat — a northern subgroup of the Mongols.

Shaman Rock (Olkhon) is one of the most mysterious of the Baikal sites. It is comprised of white marble, granite and quartz. The first explorers to reach Lake Baikal noted that Baikal Buryats traditionally feared this site. Only shamans had the right to approach this forbidden place.

Olkhon is a site of legends, rituals and miracles. Here you will walk into a meditative trance, enjoy the now, experience the real ‘core shamanism’ as it has been practised for hundreds of years .

You Should visit Baikal Lake in winter (seriously)

Fantastic grottoes on the shore, jeep or ice skating tours, ice fishing, dog sleigh rides and reassuring quiet: these are only some of the joys that make a trip to Lake Baikal in winter worthwhile.

Deep blue, inky and interspersed with innumerable air bubbles whose color is sparkling blue or blindingly white: Such is the ice of Baikal. From late January to the end of April the legendary lake is solidly frozen and is the main natural sight of the Irktusk Region.

The Sacred Sea

Russia’s vast and expansive wilderness is a place of rugged beauty, and Lake Baikal is no exception.

Up to 1637 meters deep and stretching 63 kilometers from north to south, Lake Baikal is a colossal natural wonder and as significant to local folklore as it is to science.

Baikal is truly a lake of superlatives. It is one of the biggest in the world, easily the oldest and the deepest – it is more than a mile to the bottom. With a surface area bigger than Belgium, it contains nearly one quarter of all the world's freshwater, which is more than the five American Great Lakes combined, enough, someone calculated, to meet the globe's demands for 40 years.

The water is highly oxygenated and well over half the species that inhabit it are unique. Among them are freshwater seals and a type of crab that devours everything organic, which accounts for the lake's vodka clarity.

With its unique flora and fauna and fascinating local culture, Siberia's sacred sea is well worth the journey.

The greatest train journey on Earth

Crossing seven time zones, between Moscow and Vladivostok, the 5,772-mile train track blazes a trail through some of the bleakest and most beautiful landscapes on Earth.

Completed in October 1916 – exactly 100 years ago – the Trans-Siberian Railway helped shape the Russia we know today, though its influence can be felt much further afield, not least in Japan, China and Europe.

Winston Churchill famously described Russia as “a riddle wrapped in a mystery inside an enigma” – words that still, perhaps, ring true today. A journey aboard the Trans-Siberian won’t necessarily solve that riddle, but it will certainly help make sense of this furtive nation...Read more

Siberia on a plate

Most Siberians are locavores. Living in this former wilderness, they’ve learned to eat as much locally-sourced food as possible, and today they pride themselves on the quantity and freshness of the homegrown, home-preserved, and locally-foraged dishes on their tables. Even the city-dwellers like to get out into the woods to pick berries and mushrooms in the fall, or to fish in the rivers and streams.

Enjoy an irresistable Siberian home-cooked meal: pelmeni , a shot of vodka, borscht, a shot of vodka, homemade pickles, Buryat pozy (dumplings), a shot of vodka, handmade piroshky, a shot of vodka, a shot of vodka, a shot of vodka, stroganina, slices of frozen fish (or meat) finely chopped and then heavily seasoned with salt; more vodka, zagutai made of finely cut Baikal omul; even more vodka, raskolodka...guess what... goes with vodka!
